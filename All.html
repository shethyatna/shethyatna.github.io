ALL TO KNOW ABOUT JAINISM



FOOD IN JAINISM

The Jain diet is a lacto-vegetarian diet that excludes root vegetables, certain types of fruits, and certain types of dairy products. It is also based on the principle of ahimsa, or non-violence, which means that Jains avoid foods that involve harming or killing living beings.



MONKS AND NUNS OF JAINISM

Monks and nuns in Jainism are known as mendicants. They have renounced the material world and live a life of simplicity and austerity. Their goal is to achieve moksha, or liberation from the cycle of rebirth.Jain monks and nuns wear different types of clothing depending on their sect. Svetambara monks wear white robes, while Digambara monks wear no clothing at all. Nuns of both sects wear white robes.Jain monks and nuns typically live in monasteries or convents. They spend their days studying the Jain scriptures, meditating, and performing rituals. They also travel from place to place to teach the Jain faith and to give sermons.Jain monks and nuns play an important role in the Jain community. They are responsible for preserving the Jain faith and for teaching it to future generations.

MANUSCRIPTS OF JAINISM

The Agamas: The Agamas are the canonical texts of Jainism. They contain the teachings of Mahavira, the 24th Tirthankara of Jainism.The Kalpasutra: The Kalpasutra is a historical text that contains the biographies of the 24 Tirthankaras.The Bhagavati Sutra: The Bhagavati Sutra is a philosophical text that discusses the Jain principles of non-violence, ahimsa, and karma.The Neminatha Katha: The Neminatha Katha is a narrative poem that tells the story of Neminatha, the 22nd Tirthankara of Jainism.The Kumarapala Desha: The Kumarapala Desha is a historical poem that tells the story of Kumarapala, a Jain king.

HISTORY OF JAINISM

Jainism is an ancient religion that originated in India. It is one of the oldest religions in the world, and it is estimated to have been around for over 2,500 years.The history of Jainism is divided into two periods: the ancient period and the medieval period. The ancient period began with the life of Mahavira, the 24th and last Tirthankara of Jainism. Mahavira is believed to have lived in the 6th century BCE, and he is credited with teaching the Jain path of non-violence, ahimsa, and compassion.The medieval period began with the death of Mahavira and continued until the 12th century CE. During this period, Jainism spread to other parts of India and to other countries, such as Sri Lanka and Southeast Asia. Jainism also flourished during this period, and many important Jain texts were written during this time.In the 12th century CE, Jainism began to decline in India. This was due to a number of factors, including the rise of Islam and the decline of the Jain merchant class. However, Jainism continued to be practiced in India, and it is still a thriving religion today.



PHILOSOPHY IN JAINISM

principles of non-violence, ahimsa, and compassion. Jains believe that all living things have souls, and they strive to avoid harming any living thing.One of the central concepts in Jain philosophy is the concept of karma. Karma is a law of cause and effect that states that every action has a reaction. Jains believe that the actions we take in this life determine our fate in the next life. The goal of Jainism is to achieve liberation from the cycle of birth and death, which can be achieved by following the Jain path of non-violence, ahimsa, and compassion.Another important concept in Jain philosophy is the concept of anekantavada, which means "many-sidedness." Jains believe that reality is complex and multifaceted, and that there is no single truth. They also believe that all beings are interconnected, and that we are all responsible for each other.Jain philosophy has a number of implications for our everyday lives. For example, Jains believe that we should avoid harming any living thing, even insects and plants. They also believe that we should be honest and truthful in our dealings with others. Jains also believe that we should be compassionate and understanding towards others, even those who have wronged us.

KARMA AND THEIR TYPES

Jainism teaches that there are eight types of karma that can bind the soul to the cycle of birth and death. These eight types of karma are:

  • Gyanavaraniya karma: This karma obscures the soul's knowledge and wisdom.

  • Darshanavaraniya karma: This karma obscures the soul's perception and understanding.

  • Mohaniya karma: This karma deludes the soul and leads it to make wrong choices.

  • Antaraya karma: This karma obstructs the soul's progress and prevents it from achieving liberation.

  • Ayu karma: This karma determines the soul's lifespan.

  • Naam karma: This karma determines the soul's physical appearance and body type.

  • Gotra karma: This karma determines the soul's social status and family background.

  • Vedaniya karma: This karma determines the soul's experience of pleasure and pain.



FESTIVALS

  • Paryushan: Paryushan is the most important Jain festival of the year. It is a 10-day festival that is celebrated in August or September. Paryushan is a time for Jains to focus on their spiritual practice and to reflect on their lives. Jains typically observe Paryushan by fasting, praying, and chanting mantras.

  • Mahavira Jayanti: Mahavira Jayanti is the birthday of Mahavira, the 24th and last Tirthankara of Jainism. It is celebrated in April or May. Mahavira Jayanti is a time for Jains to celebrate the life and teachings of Mahavira. Jains typically observe Mahavira Jayanti by attending religious services, singing hymns, and making donations to Jain temples.

  • Diwali: Diwali is a Hindu festival that is also celebrated by Jains. Jains celebrate Diwali as the day when Mahavira attained moksh (releave from karma cycle and ended his live ) . Jains typically observe Diwali by lighting lamps, decorating their homes, and exchanging gifts.

  • Navpad Oli: Navpad Oli is a nine-day festival that is celebrated in March or April and September or October. Navpad Oli is a time for Jains to focus on their spiritual practice and to reflect on their lives. Jains typically observe Navpad Oli by fasting, praying, and chanting mantras.


TRADITIONS

  • Non-violence (ahimsa): Non-violence is the core principle of Jainism. Jains believe that all living things have souls, and they strive to avoid harming any living thing. Jains practice non-violence in all aspects of their lives, including their diet, their clothing, and their occupations.

  • Vegetarianism: Jains are strict vegetarians. They believe that it is wrong to harm animals, even for food. Jains also avoid eating root vegetables, such as potatoes and carrots, because they believe that these vegetables are living beings.

  • Meditation: Meditation is an important practice in Jainism. Jains meditate to focus on their breath and to quiet their minds. Meditation helps Jains to develop concentration and to achieve spiritual liberation.

  • Community service: Jains believe that it is their duty to serve others. They participate in a variety of community service activities, such as providing food and shelter to the poor and sick. Also the jain give a lot of money in charity .

  • Fasting: Jains fast on a regular basis. Fasting helps Jains to cleanse their bodies and minds, and to focus on their spiritual practice.

  • Religious study: Jains study Jain scriptures and philosophy on a regular basis. Religious study helps Jains to better understand their faith and to live their lives in accordance with Jain principles.The stanzas that we learn on daily bases are called GATHAS .

  • Pilgrimage: Jains make pilgrimages to Jain temples and other holy sites. Pilgrimage helps Jains to deepen their faith and to connect with the Jain community.
